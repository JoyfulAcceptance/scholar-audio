# The Problem and Inspiration

## Writing prompt

What happens when someone still needs to read but their eyes, attention, or body need a different way to continue? Describe the manual scholarly-listening workflow that existed before Scholar Audio and the moment it became clear that raw text-to-speech was not enough.

## Ideas to consider

- Tired readers and dense papers
- Eyes needing to rest while the argument continues
- Listening while keeping the PDF available for charts, tables, equations, footnotes, and images
- “Reading with your ears” is different from dumping extracted text into a voice
- Existing manual experiments proved that local Mac voices could support a useful workflow

## Draft

Heavy-duty reading can be difficult when several articles and unfamiliar concepts must be absorbed at once. Sometimes my eyes simply need a rest, so I began looking for a way to listen to scholarly papers without paying for another service or uploading private documents to the cloud.

Before Build Week, I experimented with a manual workflow using tools already available on my Mac. With help from Codex, I extracted the text, divided papers into meaningful sections, and added pacing cues that gave listeners more breathing room around new terms and dense concepts. I could listen while keeping the PDF open to follow the argument and examine graphs or tables.

Those experiments worked, but producing each audiobook required many separate steps. Scholar Audio grew from the idea that this manual process could become one simple, repeatable local tool: give it a paper and receive a complete audiobook with clearly named chapters.
