# What Scholar Audio Does

## Writing prompt

Explain Scholar Audio to a tired reader who does not care about the implementation. What do they give it, what choices do they make, and what do they receive?

## Ideas to consider

- Drop in a text-based PDF, Markdown file, or text file
- Choose an installed Mac voice and speaking pace
- Prepare a document-specific listening sample
- Clean and restructure the document for listening
- Create named chapter tracks and one complete chaptered `.m4b` audiobook
- Download audio alone or a packet containing the original document, audiobook, and listening README
- Everything runs locally on the Mac

## Draft

Scholar Audio turns a scholarly paper into a complete, chaptered audiobook. The reader drops in a text-based PDF, Markdown file, or text file, then chooses one of the voices installed on their Mac and a comfortable listening speed. The default is 130 words per minute, selected with dense scholarly material in mind.

Before creating the full audiobook, the reader can prepare a 30-second sample taken from their own document. This lets them hear how the selected voice and pace suit the material.

When the audiobook is ready, its named chapters—such as Abstract, Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion—make it easy to move through the paper. The reader can listen while keeping the original document open for graphs, equations, tables, images, and footnotes.

The completed `.m4b` works in the reader's preferred audiobook player. Its short citation-style filename includes the author, year, and title, making it recognizable in an audio library. The reader can download the audiobook alone or a packet containing both the audio and original document.

Scholar Audio runs locally, so the reader does not have to send the paper to a cloud text-to-speech service.
